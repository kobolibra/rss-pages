Gold in the stress test

3/27/2026

How the precious metal behaves relative to the S&P 500 across different market phases

The market turbulence triggered by the Iran war has once again raised questions about gold’s role as a perceived short term safe haven.[Footnote: Financial safe havens are investments or assets that are expected to retain or increase in value during times of market turbulence.] Despite heightened geopolitical tensions, which have led to sharp declines in equity and bond markets, the gold price has recently come under noticeable pressure. Although the yellow metal had already gained around 25% in the first two months of the year - leaving prices still up year to date - it is noteworthy, in our view, that since the outbreak of the war gold has suffered just as much as other asset classes, contradicting its traditional image as a crisis hedge.

Exception or pattern? To answer this question, it is worth looking at how gold and equities have behaved across different market phases. Whether two asset classes tend to move together or in opposite directions can be measured by correlation: a value of +1 means both assets move in the same direction. A value of 0 indicates no measurable relationship, whereas a value of -1 means the two assets move exactly in opposite directions. For an asset to be considered a so-called “safe haven,” a low or negative correlation would be particularly desirable during periods of sharp equity-market losses.

The Chart of the Week shows the correlation between the daily returns of gold and the S&P 500 across different market phases, classified both by the magnitude of the S&P 500’s daily return and by two distinct time periods. We observe that in the period from 1975 to 2016, gold generally showed only a weak correlation with equity-market movements. Notably, gold tended to gain in value when equities sold off sharply (a daily loss of more than 2.5%), with an observed negative correlation of 0.13. Since 2016, however, a different pattern has emerged: a positive correlation on days with significant equity-market declines. In other words, precisely in phases when a safe-haven asset would be expected to provide stability, gold has tended to move in the same direction as equities. This parallel movement was also evident on strongly positive market days, allowing ‘diversified’ investors to benefit twice.

How can this phenomenon be explained? Why do equity investors apparently not flee into supposedly safe gold when the equity market posts significant losses? First and foremost, gold is a narrower and less liquid market segment compared with the stock or bond market. It has also become increasingly shaped by passive demand (through ETFs or rules‑based strategies). With the broad withdrawal of proprietary trading by banks and brokers, the market has lost an important shock absorber. In the past, these institutions actively traded with their own capital and helped smooth market fluctuations; many of them have since stepped back. At the same time, the share of passive investors has increased - players who tend to amplify market moves rather than cushion them. Added to this are investors operating with fixed risk budgets or leveraged positions who require liquidity quickly when margin calls occur. In short: when one asset class experiences an exceptionally sharp downturn, investors must raise cash where they can liquidate positions quickly and/or where they are sitting on gains - as has recently been the case with gold.

In this analysis, we are explicitly referring to daily movements. How gold correlates with other asset classes over longer time horizons - and which factors influence both the level and the direction of that correlation - is a different matter altogether. In the short term, gold has at times still provided diversification benefits, for example around ‘Liberation Day’ in April 2025 or during the Ukraine war in 2022 and the Covid‑19 pandemic. More recently, however, outflows from more tactical investment segments, such as retail investors or certain asset managers, have weighed on the gold market. Gold remains a valuable diversifier in our view, but its safe‑haven characteristics vary depending on the period and the event - and therefore require ongoing, up‑to‑date reassessment rather than blind reliance.
